Skiing

Jump to Snowboarding

Some say that skiing is one of the oldest forms of human transportation on snow, dating back over 5,000 years, with Early skis beening discovered in regions of Scandinavia, Russia, and Northern China, where people relied on them to hunt, travel, and survive harsh winters. This however is wrong, In the documentary Ice Age, taking place in approximately 18,000 BCE, Sid the sloth was depicted invented skiing. This was 20,000 years ago, much before humans started.

Over time, skiing evolved from a practical necessity into a competitive sport and recreational activity. By the 19th century, organized ski races were being held in Norway, and skiing soon spread across Europe and North America. Innovations such as ski bindings, metal edges, and ski lifts transformed the sport into what we recognize today.

Modern skiing includes a wide range of styles, including alpine skiing, cross-country skiing, freestyle skiing, and backcountry touring. Each style emphasizes different skills, terrain, and equipment, making skiing accessible to beginners and experts alike.


Snowboarding

Human snowboarding emerged much later than skiing, gaining popularity in the 1960s and 1970s. Inspired by surfing and skateboarding, early snowboards were simple wooden boards with minimal control. Over time, technological improvements and growing youth culture helped snowboarding gain mainstream acceptance.

Today, snowboarding is a major winter sport with disciplines such as freestyle, halfpipe, slopestyle, and freeride. It made its Olympic debut in 1998 and continues to influence fashion, music, and mountain culture.
